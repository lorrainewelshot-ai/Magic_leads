Performance Improvement Plans carry significant legal and reputational risk when the underlying issue is a functional or neurological one rather than a conduct or attitude problem. Under the Equality Act 2010, neurodivergent conditions — including ADHD, autism, dyslexia, and dyspraxia — may qualify as disabilities, triggering a duty to make reasonable adjustments before any formal process begins. Work through each point below before proceeding.

01
Has the work environment been formally reviewed for this employee?
Not a generic risk assessment — a specific review of how this person's environment supports or undermines their function. Factors include noise level, desk placement, lighting, meeting formats, and the structure of communication and task assignment.
High risk if skipped
—
—
02
Has the employee had a structured opportunity to communicate barriers?
Not a disciplinary meeting, and not simply "is there anything you want to tell us?" — a purposeful, psychologically safe conversation in which the employee could disclose difficulties, processing differences, or health factors without fear of it being used against them.
Equality Act exposure
—
—
03
Has function — not just output — been assessed?
Output metrics show what isn't happening; they don't explain why. An occupational therapy assessment examines executive function, sensory processing, and task organisation — identifying whether the gap is cognitive, environmental, motivational, or a combination.
Medium risk if skipped
—
—
04
Have reasonable adjustments been explored and documented — even if none were made?
The duty to consider adjustments exists whether or not a formal diagnosis is in place. If you knew — or ought to have known — that the employee may have a disability, the duty is engaged. Documentation of this process is your primary defence in a tribunal claim.
Equality Act exposure
—
—
05
Is the PIP process proportionate, specific, and achievable given what you now know?
Generic targets — "improve time management," "meet all deadlines" — are legally and practically weak. Targets should be specific, measurable, supported, and take into account any functional or environmental factors already identified. An unachievable PIP, or one that doesn't account for known factors, may be viewed as constructive dismissal.
High risk if generic
