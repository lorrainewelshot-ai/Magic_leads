The Employee-Workplace Alignment Programme — Manager's Resource

Performance
or Processing?

Before escalating to a PIP or formal capability process, work through these five questions. They take under two minutes — and could save you from an expensive mistake. Answer honestly based on what you've actually observed.

```
Question 1 of 5
Is the performance problem consistent across all tasks — or specific to certain types?
e.g. they struggle with planning, prioritising, or switching between tasks — but are capable or even excellent at structured, hands-on, or routine work.
Question 2 of 5
Has performance noticeably declined after a role change, promotion, or change in working conditions?
e.g. moved to hybrid/remote, changed line manager, new office layout, increased workload complexity — and they seemed to manage before this shift.
Question 3 of 5
Have you observed signs of effort and intent — but inconsistent follow-through?
e.g. they clearly care, arrive prepared or engaged, but miss deadlines, lose track of tasks, go blank under pressure, or have very good and very bad days with no obvious reason.
Question 4 of 5
Has the employee mentioned stress, overwhelm, concentration difficulties, or said they "don't know why" they're struggling?
Or have you noticed physical signs — difficulty in noisy or busy environments, avoidance of certain meeting formats, visible fatigue or distraction disproportionate to the situation?
Question 5 of 5
Has the employee ever disclosed — formally or informally — that they may be neurodivergent, or asked about workplace adjustments?
This includes ADHD, autism, dyslexia, dyspraxia — diagnosed, self-identified, or suspected. Even informal mentions carry legal weight under the Equality Act 2010.
Likely Indication
This may be a conduct or capability issue
Your answers suggest the issue is less likely to be driven by underlying functional or neurological factors. A structured performance management process — with clear expectations, support, and documentation — may be the appropriate route.

However: even where performance management is warranted, an OT assessment can still provide useful evidence and reduce tribunal risk. If the employee later discloses a diagnosis, a documented functional assessment strengthens your position considerably.
Likely Indication
The picture is mixed — proceed carefully
Some answers point to functional or processing difficulties; others suggest performance factors may also be at play. Rushing into a PIP here carries real risk — both to the employee's wellbeing and your legal exposure.

Recommended next step: commission a functional OT assessment before deciding on any formal process. This gives you an objective, clinical evidence base — and shows good faith in meeting your reasonable adjustment duty.

Strong Indication
This looks like a processing problem, not a performance problem
The pattern you've described — inconsistent output, task-specific difficulties, effort without follow-through, sensitivity to environment — is consistent with executive function or sensory processing difficulties. These are clinical presentations, not attitude problems.

Starting a PIP now would likely make things worse — and could expose your organisation to an Equality Act claim if the employee later receives a diagnosis.
```
